THE PENITENTS

Los Penitentes

Statement Begins

Los Hermanos Penitentes de la Tercera Orden de San Francisco — the Brotherhood of Penitent Brothers — is a lay Catholic confraternity that developed in New Mexico and Colorado from the late colonial period, whose practices include self-flagellation with yucca whips, carrying heavy crosses, and ritual simulations of the Crucifixion during Holy Week.

Their meeting houses — moradas — are, notably, modeled architecturally on the kiva ceremonial chambers of the indigenous Pueblo peoples of the same region. The Catholic penitential practice arrived from Spain. The sacred space in which it was practiced was built on an indigenous template.

The Penitentes represent the point where Spanish Catholic penitential theology — itself already carrying the charge of blood as sacred medium, of suffering as salvific — landed on a cultural substrate that already understood the body’s pain as a form of prayer. The autosacrifice of the Maya aristocracy drawing ropes through their tongues (Maya Blood Cosmology) and the self-flagellation of the Penitentes are not the same practice. But they are recognizable to each other in a way that purely European and purely indigenous practices would not be. See Theodicy.
